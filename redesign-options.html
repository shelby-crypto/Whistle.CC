Whistle Redesign Proposals

UX/UI analysis and 3 design directions for making Whistle (NetRef Safety) a better app for athletes, referees, and their teams. All options address the UX and content issues identified below.

UX & Content Strategy Findings

High Priority

Athlete-First Language Missing

The app uses technical moderation jargon ("pipeline runs", "classifier output", "content_action") throughout. Athletes and their managers need human language: "threats detected", "action taken", "safe to ignore". The brand name "NetRef Safety" also reads as generic — the app should speak to the athlete's experience: protecting their peace, managing their public presence, keeping their team informed.

High Priority

No Quick-Glance Safety Status

When an athlete opens the app after a game, they need ONE answer immediately: "Am I safe?" or "There are threats that need attention." The current dashboard shows raw numbers (Total Posts: 47, High Harm: 3) but doesn't synthesize this into a clear safety signal. A threat-level indicator (green/yellow/red) with a single sentence summary would serve athletes 10x better.

High Priority

Feed Shows Harmful Content by Default

The feed page displays all flagged content with the harmful text visible (though hideable per-item). For athletes dealing with online harassment, being forced to see this content causes real psychological harm. The default should be content-hidden with only metadata visible (author, risk level, action taken). Content should only be revealed intentionally.

Medium Priority

Settings Don't Persist

Auto-moderation preferences (high harm → block+delete, medium → mute, etc.) are stored only in React state. Refreshing the page loses all settings. These must be persisted to the database. This is both a UX bug and a functional gap — the AI pipeline can't actually USE these preferences for real-time moderation if they only live in the browser.

Medium Priority

No Game-Day / Event Context

Athletes experience spikes in harassment around specific events (games, controversial calls, public appearances). The "Monitoring Windows" feature addresses this but is buried in settings and feels disconnected from the main experience. It should be front-and-center with templates: "Game Night", "Post-Match", "Off-Season".

Medium Priority

Navigation Labels Are Generic

"Feed" and "Connect" don't tell athletes what these pages do. Better labels: "Threats" or "Activity" instead of "Feed"; "Accounts" instead of "Connect"; "Protection" instead of "Settings". The navigation should use the athlete's mental model, not the developer's.

Low Priority

Empty States Need Encouragement

When there are no threats, the feed shows "No items to display" — a missed opportunity to reassure the athlete. Better: "All clear — no threats detected in the last 24 hours. We're still watching." Make the absence of threats feel like the product working, not the product being empty.

Content Strategy Recommendations

Voice & Tone for Athletes

Current: Technical, detached ("Content Moderation", "Pipeline Error", "Classifier Output")
Proposed: Protective, calm, empowering. Like a trusted team member briefing you before a press conference. Serious when threats are real, reassuring when things are calm. Never alarmist, never dismissive.

Tone spectrum: Serious but warm • Formal but accessible • Matter-of-fact but caring • Confident but not aggressive

Key Copy Changes

"Content Moderation" → "Your Protection Dashboard"
"High Harm" → "Serious Threats"
"Medium Harm" → "Concerning Activity"
"Questionable" → "Worth Watching"
"Reviewed" → "Handled"
"Total Posts" → "Posts Scanned"
"Blocked Users" → "Users Blocked"
"Unreviewed" → "Needs Your Attention"
"No items to display" → "All clear — we're keeping watch"
"Feed" → "Activity"
"Connect" → "Accounts"
"Settings" → "Protection Settings"

3 Redesign Directions

Option 1

The Command Center

A calm, focused control room aesthetic. Teal-on-dark with a clean status-first layout. The dashboard opens with a single large safety status indicator, then cascades into details. Designed for the athlete's manager or agent who checks in multiple times per day.

Safety Status Hero

Athlete-First Language

All labels rewritten for clarity: "Threats", "Handled", "Watching", "Protection".

Content Hidden by Default

Athlete

Game-Day Mode Prominent

Quick-activate "Game Day" from the dashboard — heightens monitoring automatically.

Option 2

Shield & Guard

A premium, app-like experience with indigo/purple tones. Card-based layout with a personal safety score. Feels like a luxury security service. Designed for the athlete themselves — someone who wants to feel protected without diving into details.

Personal Safety Score

0-100 score that reflects your current online safety. Drops when threats increase, recovers when handled.

Copy

Passive Protection Language

"Your protection is active", "threats neutralized" — makes the athlete feel guarded without effort.

Athlete

Minimal Interaction Required

Auto-handled items are summarized, not listed. Only items needing YOUR decision are highlighted.

UX

Card-Based, Not Table-Based

Replace data tables with digestible cards. Athletes aren't reviewing spreadsheets.

Option 3

The Sideline Coach

Warm, sporty aesthetic with orange/amber accents. Conversation-style layout with AI-generated summaries. Feels like getting a briefing from your team's social media manager. Designed for athletes who want context and actionable advice, not just data.
